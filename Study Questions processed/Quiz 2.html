Good work!

At equilibrium, the forward and reverse reactions occur at the same rate. This does not mean that the concentrations of reactants and products are equal. Typically one will be significantly higher than the other. But, the relative concentrations of the reactants and products will cease to change. For example, if A and B are converted to C and D, and C and D are converted to A and B in the reverse reaction, then equilibrium is reached when the rate of conversion of A and B to C and D is the same as the rate of conversion of C and D to A and B. If the reaction of A and B to form C and D is more energetically favorable than the reaction of C and D to form A and B, equilibrium may not be reached until the concentrations of C and D are significantly higher than the concentrations of A and B. At that point, even though individual molecules of A and B may be more likely to react to form molecules of C and D, because there are so many more molecules of C and D the rate at which molecules of C and D are converted to A and B becomes the same as the rate at which molecules of A and B are converted to C and D. For example, imagine a test tube in which there are 1,000 molecules each of A and B and that 10% of them interact per minute to form C and D. That would mean that the rate of conversion of A and B to C and D is 100 per minute. If the there are 10,000 molecules each of C and D in the test tube and 1% of them react per minute to form A and B, the rate of conversion of C and D to A and B would also be 100 per minute, indicating that the system is at equilibrium. If you removed some of the molecules of C and D from the test tube, right after you removed the C and D the rate of conversion of A and B to C and D would be unchanged, but the rate of conversion of C and D would decrease. So, the system would no longer be at equilibrium as the rate of conversion of A and B to C and D would be higher than the rate of conversion of C and D to A and B. Because of this, the relative concentrations of A and B versus C and D would also be changing, with C and D increasing relative to A and B. Eventually, the concentrations of C and D would increase enough and the concentrations of A and B would decrease enough so that the rates of the forward and reverse reactions would become equal, meaning that equilibrium would be re-established.

It is not possible to PROVE that a hypothesis is correct. Instead the results of tests (experiments) may only show that a particular hypothesis is false. For example, if your hypothesis for why a flashlight isnt working is that the batteries are dead, you might predict that replacing the batteries will fix the flashlight. If you then test your prediction by replacing the batteries and find that the flashlight still does not work, you have disproven your original prediction that replacing the batteries would fix the flashlight and have thus shown your hypothesis to be incorrect (false). However, if the flashlight works after you replace the batteries, that finding does not prove that your hypothesis was correct. This is because you can still come up with alternative hypotheses for why the flashlight now works. For example, you could hypothesize that the reason the flashlight wasnt working previously was because the original batteries were inserted incorrectly. Then, when you replaced the batteries, you inserted the new batteries correctly and so fixed the flashlight. If that is the case, then your original hypothesis that the old batteries were dead would be false. The bottom line is that we can never rule out that there might be a different hypothesis that also explains all of the available experimental results and that is actually the correct hypothesis.
